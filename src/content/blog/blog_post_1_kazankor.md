# Kazankor: A World With a Hidden Premise

I've been running a D&D campaign in a setting I've been building for years called Kazankor. The thing that took the longest to settle wasn't a particular system or a particular city — it was the premise underneath everything.

Here's the premise.

The gods of this world are farming it. They have been doing this for a very long time. The world produces a substance called Tamutash — a fine indestructible dust that bonds permanently with rune-inscription and makes it work. Tamutash only forms on a substrate called Agyntash, which the gods seed into the world themselves. Every ten or twenty thousand years, the gods engineer a massive civilizational war, because war spikes the use of raw divine energy and accelerates the Tamutash harvest. After the war ends, the gods harvest what was made, the civilization above falls, and a new one starts growing on top of the ruins.

The current civilization has no idea this is happening.

That premise informs almost every other decision in the setting. The Forgotten Arteries — the underground network the players spend a lot of time in — exist because each previous civilization's cities are still down there, preserved by old magic, layered on top of each other like geological strata. The pantheon's behaviour makes sense once you accept that the gods are running a long-term economic operation rather than ruling anything. The races include things that survived the last reset by going underground. The Illumined Monarchs are mortals who ascended to godhood; the process is a deliberate one the harvest gods allow, because ascended mortals work for them.

I designed it this way for a specific reason. Most D&D worlds have evil that the players can defeat. Kazankor has a system the players can't defeat — they can only understand it, work around it, or join it. That changes what a campaign is about. The players are not heroes saving the world; they are people who have noticed something other people haven't, and they have to decide what to do about it.

The premise also generates more questions than it answers, which I think is what a world should do. Why did this start? Did all the gods agree to it, or did some of them get outvoted? What happens if a civilization figures it out and resists? What does it mean to ascend to godhood inside a system like this? I have answers for some of these and not for others, and the players are slowly finding the rest themselves through play.
